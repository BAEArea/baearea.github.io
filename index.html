Historical Connections

Teddy Roosevelt, one of the most environmentally progressive presidents created numerous National Parks across the United States. These National Parks were sanctuaries where development was illegal. The byproduct of a developmentally protected land was an area where animal life could prosper. For example in The Teddy Roosevelt National Park there was an abundance of endangered animals prospering. These animals include, but are not limited to: Bison, hundreds of different birds, reptiles, and mammals.

Teddy Roosevelt's approach of effectively creating animal sanctuaries would be a worthwhile approach for the Bay Area. In fact, in shoreline these areas already exist and are helpful at maintaining animal populations.

One of the most influential pieces of endangered act legislature was the Endangered Species Act. The first step of the legislature, passed in 1973, declared the list of endangered animals. This was revolutionary because it was the first time they officially categorized animals into endangered and not endangered. To categorize animals we still use this list today. The Endangered Species Act also outlined a recovery plan focused on an objective, systematic way to increase population of animals.

More related to California, the California Endangered Species Act vows to protect any species that is endangered or facing a serious decline in population. This law circumvents a reliance on taxes but instead relies on fees. If any animals are ever threatened then “the California Department of Fish and Wildlife will work with all interested persons, agencies and organizations to protect and preserve such sensitive resources and their habitats” according to the legislature. The law represents a noticeable uprise of community interest in protecting species, ensuring that everything will be done in power to ensure the Californian species will survive.

California Endangered Species Act

“all native species of fishes, amphibians, reptiles, birds, mammals, invertebrates, and plants, and their habitats, threatened with extinction and those experiencing a significant decline which, if not halted, would lead to a threatened or endangered designation, will be protected or preserved”

Protects and preserves all Californian species that are exhibiting some sort of decline.

Federal Endangered Species Act

“When Congress passed the Endangered Species Act (ESA) in 1973, it recognized that our rich natural heritage is of “esthetic, ecological, educational, recreational, and scientific value to our Nation and its people.” It further expressed concern that many of our nation’s native plants and animals were in danger of becoming extinct.”

“Under the ESA, species may be listed as either endangered or threatened. “Endangered” means a species is in danger of extinction throughout all or a significant portion of its range. “Threatened” means a species is likely to become endangered within the foreseeable future. All species of plants and animals, except pest insects, are eligible for listing as endangered or threatened. For the purposes of the ESA, Congress defined species to include subspecies, varieties, and, for vertebrates, distinct population segments.”

Solutions

  • Keep Pets Inside

    Cats, both house pets and feral, can kill more than a million songbirds a year. We have documented depredation of endangered clapper rails, snowy plovers, and California least terns that were killed by cats.

  • Don't Litter

    Trash makes its way into marshes and streams and into the diet of animals.

  • Follow Signs

    One way we protect animals is to keep people and their pets from disturbing sensitive areas. For example when Snowy Plovers are nesting, we put up cable fencing to prevent people from entering the nesting areas and inadvertently stepping on camouflaged nests.

  • Be Active

    Get outside and learn about local species in your area.

  • Support the Wildlife

    Help with restoration at a local Refuge, park and even your backyard. Creating wildlife friendly gardens for Monarchs in your backyard or at school not only helps Monarchs, but also other native butterflies, birds and insects.

  • Become an Advocate

    By voting for Measure AA, Bay Area residents said the health of the Bay Area was important.

  • What role does the San Francisco Zoo have with endangered animals?
    The SF Zoo, as a member of the Association of Zoos and Aquariums (AZA), participates in Species Survival Programs (SSPs) for the endangered animals we exhibit and care for. Here is a link to info on SSPs: https://www.aza.org/ssp-population-sustainability And, here is a link to descriptions of other conservation efforts we’re involved with: http://www.sfzoo.org/conservation/overview.htm
  • What are the most notable endangered species in the bay area today? Do you foresee their population statuses improving? Declining?
    A list of Bay Area endangered species can be found here: http://www.sfbaywildlife.info/species/endangered.htm The SF Zoo has had a very successful bald eagle breed-for-release program. Bald eagles are making a come-back in the Bay Area. We are also actively working to restore habitat in the Bay Area to protect native species. We are hopeful that this work will help save populations of San Francisco garter snake and red-legged frog.
  • What are the main causes that are driving these animals extinct? Who do you think are the most responsible for these causes?
    Habitat degradation, fragmentation and destruction are the primary reasons for loss of species in this area. Human overpopulation is a major factor in loss of habitat for native species.
  • What would be the consequences if these animals went extinct?
    Since all wildlife is interconnected, losing species will slowly destroy our ecosystem. We are losing pollinators, and smaller prey species. These losses work their way up the food chain.
  • How can the average person ameliorate the population of local endangered species?
    We each can all help by spreading the word about the importance of conservation, by educating others. We can also act as role models; practice what we preach by demonstrating our commitment to conservation: reduce, reuse, recycle, restore. And we can support organizations that are doing good in the world.

Rachel Tertes, USFWS Wildlife Biologist

  • Why does the Don Edwards San Francisco Bay National Wildlife Refuge build refuges for wildlife?
    Our refuge mission is to preserve and enhance wildlife habitat; protect migratory birds and threatened and endangered species; and provide opportunities for wildlife-oriented recreation and nature study for the surrounding communities. We are the first Urban national wildlife refuge and our creation was largely the result of grassroots efforts by the local community to protect the San Francisco Bay ecosystem.
  • Why is it human’s duty to protect animal habitats and threatened animals?
    For the most part, human's are the reason these animals and habitats are declining. In our case, the San Francisco Bay used to be surrounded by a large network of wetlands and tidal marsh spanning 190,000 acres. As people started moving to California, they developed infrastructure, filled marshes for housing and airports, diked marshes for salt production and kept chipping away at the marshes around the Bay. Today, there is only roughly 40,000 acres, just 15% of the original acreage, of tidal marsh left and the plants and animals have had to struggle to fit in a smaller foot print. The smaller foot print means increased stressors such as edge effects, predators, disturbance, and changed water regimes. We have the responsibility and the ability to not only protect but restore these tidal marshes to benefit plants and animals, improve water quality and even address climate change. We are fortunate to be part of the South Bay Salt Pond Restoration Project, the largest wetland restoration project on the West coast which is working towards these goals.
  • Is there anything else we should know about this topic?
    By protecting habitat, entire communities of animals and plants can be protected together. To protect habitats, we must work together with federal, state, and local agencies, NGOs and local communities to garner support, participation and collectively provide creative solutions.
